Luck of the draw

Before any team picks where to sleep, the draw deals them a geography. Some groups are tight clusters; others are flung across the continent. This is pure luck — how far apart a team's three host cities are, measured with no base-camp choices involved. The wider the spread, the harder the trip was always going to be. Three patterns show up: 27 teams play three different cities; 7 play one city twice but non-consecutively — there, away, then back (marked *); and 14 play one city twice back-to-back, a genuine stay-put break (marked **). For the repeats, the spread counts that city's distance for both visits, since it's a trip they make either way.

Method & sources

The travel model

Each team's total is the sum of three distances: from its base camp to the venue of each of its three group-stage matches. It deliberately makes no assumptions about routing or return trips — it's simply how far each match is from home, added together. If you want to picture round trips, double it; if a team stays regional between matches, the real figure is lower. This measure stays out of that debate.

When two of a team's matches are at the same venue, that distance appears twice in the sum (marked ×2), because they make that trip for both matches. The measure captures the spread of a team's assignments from its home base, not the precise journey it flies.

Distance

Great-circle (haversine) distance between coordinates, in kilometres. This is straight-line "as the crow flies" — real routings are slightly longer.

Flight hours

An illustrative estimate: 0.75 h + km / 800 for each of the three base-to-venue legs, summed. It assumes every leg is flown. Several short legs (e.g. Mexico City–Guadalajara at ~470 km) would realistically be ground travel, so treat flight hours as illustrative rather than literal.

Time zones

The maximum number of time-zone boundaries crossed on any single leg, DST-adjusted for June 2026. Because the host geography is US-heavy, most teams stay within one or two zones — so this metric separates teams less sharply than distance.

The floor & penalty

The "floor" is the route distance a team would face if based at the geographic centroid of its three venues — the best feasible base camp given its fixtures, using the same routing rule. The "penalty" is actual minus floor. Negative penalties mean a base camp beat the centroid by hugging clustered venues.

Sources

Match schedule: official FIFA fixture list (uploaded), cross-checked against the published NBC Sports schedule. Base camps: U.S. Soccer's confirmed Team Base Camp list. Stadium and base-camp coordinates geocoded to venue/town centroids — distances are precise to within a few kilometres, not metres.

A note on framing: this analysis measures real base-camp-to-match travel, which blends the draw with each team's own siting decision. It is not a measure of which teams got the hardest schedule — section 03 separates those.
